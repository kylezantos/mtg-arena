# Secrets of Strixhaven: Pre-Release Research Brief

> **TL;DR — Pick Witherbloom (Black/Green) or Lorehold (White/Red) for your prerelease kit.** Both colleges have the most beginner-friendly mechanics. Witherbloom's Infusion rewards you for gaining life (something that happens naturally), while Lorehold's Flashback lets you cast spells from your graveyard (built-in card advantage). For your commander precon, grab **Lorehold Spirit** — it's easy to pilot, has great reprint value ($189), and teaches you graveyard synergies that transfer to other formats.
>
> If you want the best financial value regardless of gameplay, Silverquill Influence has $214 in reprints including Land Tax ($25 card).

---

## What is Pre-Release?

Pre-release is a casual, in-store event where you get to play with the new set before it officially launches. Here's what to expect:

- **Dates:** April 17-23, 2026 (official release is April 24)
- **Format:** Sealed — you open 6 booster packs, then build a 40-card deck from what you pull
- **NOT draft** — in draft you pick cards one at a time from shared packs. Sealed means you just open your own packs and build. Some stores run side-draft events too, but the main prerelease is sealed
- **Vibe:** Casual and beginner-friendly. People are there to have fun and see new cards. Don't stress about winning

### What's In Your Prerelease Kit

When you sign up, you **choose a college** (more on that below). Your kit contains:

| Item | Details |
|------|---------|
| 5 Play Boosters | Random cards from the full set |
| 1 College-Seeded Booster | Cards weighted toward your chosen college's two colors |
| 1 Foil Promo | A random rare or mythic rare in traditional foil |
| 1 Deck Box | Themed to your college |
| 1 Spindown Die | For tracking life total, themed to your college |

The college you pick affects that 1 seeded booster — it gives you a head start in those colors, but 5 of your 6 boosters are random. You can absolutely build a deck in different colors if you open better cards elsewhere.

## The Five Colleges

Strixhaven is a magical university on the plane of Arcavios. Each college represents a two-color pair with its own philosophy and playstyle.

### Quick Comparison

| College | Colors | Mechanic | Playstyle | Beginner Friendliness |
|---------|--------|----------|-----------|-----------------------|
| **Witherbloom** | Black/Green | Infusion | Lifegain + sacrifice value engine | Great — forgiving, hard to run out of gas |
| **Lorehold** | White/Red | Flashback | Graveyard recursion, cast spells twice | Great — built-in card advantage |
| **Silverquill** | White/Black | Repartee | Targeted removal + aggressive tempo | Medium — rewards good targeting decisions |
| **Prismari** | Blue/Red | Opus | Big splashy spells with bonus effects | Medium — needs mana management skills |
| **Quandrix** | Blue/Green | Increment | +1/+1 counters, creatures snowball | Medium — needs sequencing awareness |

### Witherbloom (Black/Green) — "Growth Through Decay"

**Mechanic: Infusion** — Your cards get bonus effects if you gained life at any point during the turn. It doesn't matter how much life you gained — even 1 life triggers it. This is forgiving because lots of cards naturally gain you life (lifelink creatures, drain effects, etc.).

**Why it's great for beginners:**
- Lifegain keeps you alive while you learn
- The sacrifice theme generates value from creatures that would die anyway
- Infusion triggers are easy to track — "did I gain life this turn? Yes/no"
- Black and Green have strong creatures AND removal

**In sealed:** Witherbloom tends to be consistent. Black gives you creature removal (kill spells), Green gives you big creatures. The lifegain from Infusion cards helps you stabilize when behind.

### Lorehold (White/Red) — "Leave No Knowledge Behind"

**Mechanic: Flashback** — Instants and sorceries with Flashback can be cast a second time from your graveyard (at a different cost, then they exile). This means your spells are effectively worth double.

**Why it's great for beginners:**
- Flashback is one of Magic's most beloved mechanics — simple to understand, powerful to use
- You get to cast your best spells twice, which means more margin for error
- White has good small creatures and removal; Red has direct damage and aggressive creatures
- The graveyard-as-resource theme teaches you to think about cards beyond just your hand

**In sealed:** Lorehold gives you natural card advantage. Even if your opponent has better individual cards, casting everything twice can overwhelm them. The Spirits subtheme also gives you evasive flyers.

### Silverquill (White/Black) — "Sharp Style. Sharper Wit."

**Mechanic: Repartee** — Triggers whenever you cast an instant or sorcery that targets a creature. Each Repartee card has a different bonus effect. Only triggers once per spell, even if the spell targets multiple creatures.

**Why it's solid (but slightly harder):**
- White/Black has the best removal in the set — tons of unconditional kill spells
- Repartee rewards you for using your removal, which you should be doing anyway
- Aggressive creatures + removal is a proven winning formula in sealed
- The challenge: you need to target wisely. Repartee only fires on spells that target creatures specifically

**In sealed:** Silverquill is the "play good cards, kill their good cards" college. If you open strong removal, it's arguably the strongest college. But it requires making decisions about what to kill and when.

### Prismari (Blue/Red) — "Express Yourself with the Elements"

**Mechanic: Opus** — Triggers when you cast instants or sorceries. Every Opus card has a base effect, plus a bonus effect that only kicks in if you spent 5 or more mana on the triggering spell.

**Why it's trickier for beginners:**
- The 5-mana threshold means your best effects come later in the game
- Blue/Red tends to be more spell-heavy and creature-light, which is harder to balance in sealed
- You need to manage your mana carefully to hit those big Opus triggers
- When it works, it's spectacular — huge spells with massive bonus effects

**In sealed:** Prismari wants to survive the early game and then take over with expensive, game-ending spells. This is a viable strategy but harder to execute for a newer player because you need to know when to hold mana vs. play something.

### Quandrix (Blue/Green) — "Math is Magic"

**Mechanic: Increment** — Whenever you cast a spell, if the mana you spent is greater than a creature's power or toughness, put a +1/+1 counter on that creature. This is checked on both the trigger and resolution.

**Why it's trickier for beginners:**
- You need to track power/toughness vs. mana spent, which adds mental overhead
- The snowball potential is high (creatures keep growing), but you need to sequence spells correctly
- Blue/Green can struggle with removal — you're relying on having bigger creatures instead
- Counter tracking gets complex with multiple Increment creatures

**In sealed:** Quandrix wants to build up creatures over several turns. If your opponent doesn't have removal, your creatures become unstoppable. But if they do, you can get blown out after investing several turns growing a creature.

## Set-Wide Mechanics (All Colleges)

These mechanics appear across all colors, not tied to a specific college:

### Prepared

Some creatures enter the battlefield "Prepared" with a specific spell linked to them. While the creature is on the battlefield and still Prepared, you can cast a free copy of that linked spell from exile. Once you cast it (or the creature leaves play), the Prepared status is gone.

**What this means for you:** Prepared creatures are premium picks in sealed. You're getting a creature AND a free spell — incredible value. Always play your Prepared creatures.

### Paradigm

Appears on sorceries. When you cast a Paradigm spell, it resolves normally, then exiles itself. After that, at the beginning of each of your first main phases, you can cast a free copy. It keeps repeating every turn for free.

**What this means for you:** Paradigm cards are bombs (extremely powerful cards). Even a mediocre effect becomes ridiculous when it happens every turn for free. Draft/build around these.

### Converge

A returning mechanic. Effects scale based on how many different colors of mana you used to cast the spell (0-5). Mostly relevant if you splash a third color.

**What this means for you:** In a two-color deck, Converge cards max out at 2. Don't warp your mana base to hit 3+ colors unless the payoff is enormous.

### Mystical Archive

A bonus sheet of iconic instants and sorceries with special art treatments. One appears in every booster pack. These are powerful, well-known spells with new artwork.

**What this means for you:** You'll open some of these. They're legal in your sealed deck. Some are very powerful — treat them like regular cards when deck-building.

## Sealed Deck Building 101

You'll have about 30 minutes to build your deck after opening packs. Here's how:

### Step 1: Sort by Color

Separate all your cards into piles by color (White, Blue, Black, Red, Green, Multicolor, Colorless/Lands). This gives you a visual of what you have the most of.

### Step 2: Identify Your Best Cards

Look for:
- **Bombs** — Cards that win the game if unanswered (big creatures, Paradigm cards, Prepared creatures with great spells)
- **Removal** — Cards that kill or neutralize your opponent's creatures (destroy, exile, damage-based removal, bounce spells)
- **Evasion** — Creatures with flying, menace, or trample that are hard to block

### Step 3: Pick Two Colors

Your deck should be exactly **two colors**. Pick the two colors where you have:
1. The best bombs and removal
2. Enough playable cards (you need ~23 non-land cards)
3. A good mix of creatures and spells

Your seeded booster gives you a head start in your college's colors, but don't force it if you opened better cards elsewhere.

### Step 4: Build to 40 Cards

The magic formula for sealed:

| Category | Count | Notes |
|----------|-------|-------|
| Creatures | 14-17 | Your primary way to win and block |
| Non-creature spells | 6-9 | Removal, combat tricks, card draw |
| Lands | 17 | Always 17. Split roughly even between your two colors, leaning toward whichever color has more cards |
| **Total** | **40** | Never more, never less |

### Step 5: Check Your Mana Curve

"Mana curve" means the spread of mana costs in your deck. You want:
- A few cards at 1-2 mana (so you have something to do early)
- The bulk at 3-4 mana (your core plays)
- A few finishers at 5-6 mana (your game-enders)
- Maybe 1 card at 7+ mana (only if it's a bomb)

If everything costs 5+, you'll die before casting anything. If everything costs 1-2, you'll run out of gas.

### Step 6: You Can Rebuild!

This is crucial: **you can rebuild your deck between rounds.** If your deck feels terrible after game 1, swap in different colors or cards from your pool. Every card you didn't put in your deck is your sideboard and is always available.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Playing 3+ colors without fixing** — Stick to 2 colors. Splashing a third is risky without mana fixing (dual lands, artifacts that make any color)
- **Too many expensive cards** — If your average mana cost is above 3.5, cut something expensive
- **Not enough creatures** — Spells are great but creatures win games. Aim for at least 14
- **Playing more than 40 cards** — Every card above 40 makes your deck worse by diluting your best cards
- **Ignoring removal** — Even mediocre removal is important. You need ways to deal with your opponent's threats

- Endless upgrade paths with cards from every era of Magic

## My Recommendation For You

Given that you're about a week into MTG and coming from Arena:

### For Prerelease: Pick Witherbloom or Lorehold

**Witherbloom (Black/Green)** if you like:
- Playing creatures that generate value when they die
- Having a safety net (lifegain keeps you in games)
- A "grind them out" playstyle where you win through incremental advantage
- Infusion is the easiest mechanic to track — just "did I gain life? Yes = bonus"

**Lorehold (White/Red)** if you like:
- Aggressive strategies with a backup plan
- Getting double value from your spells (Flashback)
- Flying creatures (Spirits) that can attack over blockers
- A mechanic you may already know from Arena (Flashback has been in many sets)

### For Commander: Start with Lorehold Spirit

It's the best teaching deck of the cycle. The gameplay patterns are clear, the power level is respectable, and the reprint value ($189) means you're getting your money's worth even if you eventually take it apart for other decks.

If you're buying two: pair it with **Silverquill Influence** for the best reprint value and a completely different playstyle to learn from.

### Why Not the Others?

- **Prismari** requires patience and mana management that's harder when you're still learning sequencing
- **Quandrix** is powerful but the Increment math adds cognitive load during a long event day
- **Silverquill** is great once you know what to target, but Repartee rewards experience with the format

## Practical Tips for Your First Pre-Release

### What to Bring
- **Pen and paper** (or a life counter app) for tracking life totals
- **Sleeves** — card sleeves protect your cards and make shuffling easier. Any basic sleeves work (~$5 at the store)
- **Water and snacks** — events can run 4-5 hours
- **A good attitude** — you will lose games. Everyone does. It's the best way to learn

### What to Expect
1. You'll pick up your prerelease kit at the store
2. Everyone opens packs at the same time
3. You get ~30 minutes to build your 40-card deck
4. You play 3-4 rounds of best-of-3 (Swiss format, so you play all rounds even if you lose)
5. Most stores give out prize packs based on record, but everyone gets to play every round

### Arena Skills That Transfer
- **Combat math** — attack/block decisions work the same way
- **Mana curve awareness** — you already understand why curve matters from Arena
- **Card evaluation** — your instinct for what's "good" from Arena drafting applies here
- **Stack and priority** — if you understand instants and responses on Arena, you're ahead of most new paper players

### Arena Skills That Don't Transfer
- **Auto-tapping** — in paper, YOU choose which lands to tap. This matters a lot. Think about what you might want to cast next before tapping
- **Triggers** — Arena handles triggers automatically. In paper, you need to remember your own triggers (like Infusion or Repartee). If you forget, you miss it
- **Declaring blockers** — there's no "auto-assign damage" in paper. You need to explicitly say how damage is assigned to multiple blockers

### Ask for Help

Prerelease events have a judge or experienced player who can answer rules questions. **Don't be afraid to ask.** Everyone started somewhere, and the MTG community at prereleases is overwhelmingly welcoming to new players.
